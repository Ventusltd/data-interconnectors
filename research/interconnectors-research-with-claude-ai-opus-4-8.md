# Interconnectors Research with Claude AI Opus 4.8

Original research question: Following the Sun: Can a Planetary Power Grid Move Solar Across Time Zones?

## TL;DR

- **The physics is proven and operating today at continental scale** — China’s Changji–Guquan ±1,100 kV line already moves 12 GW (12,000 MW) some 3,300 km across roughly two of China’s geographic time zones, and Brazil’s Belo Monte links carry Amazon hydro ~2,500 km to Rio. But **no intercontinental, ocean-crossing, time-zone-bridging link has yet been built**, and the grandest visions (a true “Global Grid”) remain conceptual.
- **Moving solar westward to follow demand into darkness is real and valuable but bounded**: peer-reviewed work (Chatzivasileiadis/Ernst/Andersson; MacDonald/NOAA; Guo et al.) shows interconnection smooths the diurnal and weather cycles, cuts storage needs, and can lower system cost — yet the marginal gains and the economics weaken as cheap batteries handle the daily solar shift locally.
- **The binding constraints are political, financial and security-related, not physical.** Desertec collapsed, Xlinks was rejected by the UK in June 2025, the Asia Super Grid is geopolitically dead, and GEIDCO’s planet-spanning grid is aspirational. Long-distance transmission will keep growing *within* large countries; cross-border “supergrids” advance only where trust, governance and money align.

## Key Findings

1. **The concept is serious and well-published.** The foundational paper is Chatzivasileiadis, Ernst & Andersson, “The Global Grid,” *Renewable Energy* 57 (2013) 372–383. It argues that because demand peaks at different clock times across time zones, intercontinental links let a sunlit/peaking region export to a dark/off-peak one, and that price arbitrage can help pay for the cables. They calculate a 3,000 MW, ±800 kV, 5,500 km submarine HVDC cable delivers power at roughly €0.017–0.025/kWh, and note peak/off-peak spreads exceed €30/MWh on EPEX and can top US$100/MWh on PJM.
1. **HVDC/UHVDC is the enabling technology.** DC avoids the reactive-power, skin-effect and charging-current losses that make long AC lines — and especially long submarine AC cables — impractical. The break-even distance where HVDC beats HVAC overhead is roughly 500–800 km (and under ~100 km for submarine). UHVDC (±800 kV and ±1,100 kV) loses on the order of 1.5–3% per 1,000 km; converter stations add roughly 0.6–0.7% each. To shift power one hour of solar time you must move it roughly 1,000–1,500 km east–west; to bridge several hours, 3,000–4,000+ km.
1. **China is the proof case.** State Grid Corporation of China has built ~40,000 km of UHVDC since 2010 — far more than the rest of the world combined — moving western solar, wind and hydro to eastern load centres across multiple geographic time zones (China uses one official clock).
1. **Brazil shows it works in another continental democracy** — Belo Monte’s two ±800 kV bipoles move Amazon hydro to the southeast over ~2,000–2,500 km.
1. **The US has the fragmentation problem but also the studies.** Its grid is split into three asynchronous interconnections (Eastern, Western, ERCOT-Texas) for historical/regulatory reasons, joined only by small DC ties. NREL’s Interconnections Seam Study and MacDonald’s NOAA study quantify large benefits from a national HVDC overlay that follows weather and solar across four time zones.
1. **Russia built the original supergrid** — the Soviet/Russian Unified Energy System spans the most time zones of any grid — but the geopolitically ambitious Asia Super Grid linking Russia, Mongolia, China, Korea and Japan is now dead.
1. **The boldest cross-time-zone projects have mostly stalled**: Sun Cable (Australia→Singapore) survives but is years behind schedule; Xlinks (Morocco→UK) was rejected by the UK government in June 2025; Desertec collapsed in 2014.
1. **Honest critiques are strong**: huge capital cost, geopolitical dependency and the “energy weapon” risk, single-points-of-failure, governance/sovereignty problems, and — increasingly — the argument that plummeting battery costs handle the daily solar-to-night shift more cheaply and securely than thousands of kilometres of cable.

## Details

### 1. The core concept and its serious proponents

The intellectual anchor is **“The Global Grid” (Chatzivasileiadis, Ernst & Andersson, *Renewable Energy* 57 (2013) 372–383)**, which proposes connecting the world’s power systems into one HVDC-backboned network to harvest remote renewables. Its central time-zone argument is explicit: *“when the night – and the electricity consumption – falls in Europe, in America it is still noon and the power consumption is at its peak. Interconnections between the two continents can take advantage of the total available power and transfer it where it is needed.”*  The authors argue a wind farm in Greenland equidistant from Europe and North America could “sell its produced power always at peak price (50% of the day to Europe and 50% of the day to North America),”  raising revenue ~31–33%.

They cost a 3,000 MW, ±800 kV, 5,500 km submarine HVDC cable at roughly €0.017–0.025/kWh delivered (falling to ~€0.013/kWh at 4,400 km), against capital costs of ~€1.15–1.8 million/km plus ~€300 million per converter station, with a 40-year life and 3% discount rate. They explicitly tie arbitrage to paying for the line: *“the cable could be a competitive option for participating in the daily spot auctions, where the difference between peak and off-peak prices exceeds €30/MWh… and can surpass US$100/MWh (e.g., PJM Interconnection).”* They cite the NorNed cable earning €50 million in its first two months (~12% of invested capital) as real-world evidence that arbitrage revenue is substantial. They envision gradual development from regional reinforcement → inter-regional HVDC (up to ~2,000 km) → intercontinental links → a meshed Global Grid governed by a proposed “Global Regulator” and “Global System Operator.” Crucially, they concede the limits of the arbitrage case: *“With the increasing integration of renewable energy sources, which essentially have a near-zero marginal cost… the opportunities for price arbitrage could disappear”* for long-term contracts and day-ahead markets, leaving intra-day spot markets as the main profit source.

The **most institutionally muscular proponent is China’s GEIDCO** (Global Energy Interconnection Development and Cooperation Organization), founded March 2016 and chaired by **Liu Zhenya**, former head of State Grid. GEIDCO’s “Global Energy Interconnection” (GEI) envisions linking every continent except Antarctica with UHV lines — Liu has described 9 latitudinal and 9 longitudinal lines, ~180,000 km, connecting 100+ countries by 2050. Its explicit logic includes “energy storage across time and space boundaries” — coordinating time-zone, seasonal and resource differences — and GEIDCO frames GEI as turning the 12-hour New York–Beijing offset into an advantage.

Academic modelling broadly supports directional benefits while disagreeing on magnitude. **Guo et al., “Implications of intercontinental renewable electricity trade for energy systems and emissions,” *Nature Energy* 7 (2022) 1144–1156** (co-authored with GEIDCO), finds that “renewable electricity trade across large world regions via the underlying UHVDC interconnection can boost renewable electricity production and reduce 2020–2100 cumulative CO2 emissions from the power sector up to 9.8%,” with transmission investment “offset in the long term by reduced investments in other electricity-generation options, including nuclear and storage.” A 2025 *Nature Communications* paper finds a globally interconnected solar-wind system “could generate ~3.1 times the projected 2050 global electricity demand,” with “the solar-wind penetration… achieved using only 29.4% of the highest potential, with a 15.6% reduction in initial investment compared to a strategy without interconnection.” **Gregor Czisch’s** cost-optimised modelling of a Europe/North-Africa renewable supergrid is the foundational European antecedent.

### 2. The transmission technology

**Why DC beats AC over long distances:** AC suffers reactive-power losses, the skin effect and (in cables) charging-current losses that grow with length; submarine AC is impractical beyond ~60–100 km.  HVDC loads conductors to their thermal limit, needs fewer conductors,  and has a smaller right-of-way. The cost penalty is the converter stations at each end, so HVDC wins only beyond a break-even distance — about 500–800 km for overhead lines, under ~100 km for submarine.

**Losses:** The US EIA and others put HVDC total losses around 3–5% including converters; UHVDC overhead lines lose roughly 3% (sometimes cited ~2.6%) per 1,000 km,  with each converter station adding ~0.6–0.7%. State Grid reports that moving from ±800 kV to ±1,100 kV cut per-1,000-km line loss from about 2.8% to roughly 1.5%. The Global Grid paper notes a 6,000 km HVDC line can have better round-trip efficiency than pumped-hydro or compressed-air storage — a key point for the “transmission as storage substitute” argument.

**Voltage/distance/capacity records:** ±800 kV UHVDC entered service in China in 2010;  ±1,100 kV (Changji–Guquan) in 2018–2019 is the highest-voltage, highest-capacity (12 GW), longest (~3,300 km) UHVDC ever built.

**Time-zone arithmetic:** One hour of clock/solar time ≈ 15° of longitude ≈ ~1,000–1,500 km at mid-latitudes, so a 3,000–4,000 km line can shift solar output across 2–4 hours of the diurnal demand curve — enough to push afternoon solar into evening peaks a continent away.

### 3. China — the clearest real-world example

**Changji–Guquan ±1,100 kV UHVDC** (a.k.a. Zhundong–Wannan/South Anhui): ~3,293 km (cited up to 3,324 km) from Changji, Xinjiang to Guquan, Anhui, crossing Gansu, Ningxia, Shaanxi and Henan; 12,000 MW capacity; commissioned 2018–2019; cost ~CNY 40.7 bn (~US$5.9 bn). The world’s first 1,100 kV line — a 50% capacity increase over ±800 kV and an extension of practical distance from ~2,000 km to >3,000 km. It can deliver ~66 TWh/year  and had delivered ~300 TWh by recent reporting; Hitachi Energy (formerly ABB) and Siemens supplied converter valves, transformers and other key equipment.

**Earlier ±800 kV landmarks:** Xiangjiaba–Shanghai (the world’s first UHVDC, 2010; ~1,980 km; 6.4–7.2 GW; Sichuan hydro to Shanghai) and Jinping–Sunan (2012; 7.2 GW; Yalong River hydro to Jiangsu, through eight provinces).

**Time-zone effect:** China spans about five geographic time zones but uses a single official time. UHV lines exploit the fact that when the sun has set in the east it still shines in the west, and that distant regions sit under different weather systems — letting the grid even out solar/wind variability and, in State Grid’s framing, avoid building some storage.

### 4. Russia — the original supergrid, spanning the most time zones

Russia’s grid is the historical proof that a single synchronous/interconnected system can span a continent. It descends from the 1920 **GOELRO** electrification plan; the Soviet **Unified Energy System (UES)** progressively merged regional systems (the first 220 kV intersystem line, Dnipro–Donbass, came in 1940), and Comecon’s “Mir” system synchronised Eastern Europe.  Today the **IPS/UPS** is a wide-area synchronous grid across former-Soviet states.  Russia’s UES comprises seven interconnected power systems (Centre, Northwest, Middle Volga, South, Urals, Siberia, East),  spanning roughly seven-to-ten time zones, explicitly exploiting the fact that the daily load peak shifts across the country to optimise generation use.  (Russia spans 11 time zones overall; the Far East/East IPS operates largely in isolation.)

The **Asia Super Grid** — proposed by SoftBank’s **Masayoshi Son** via his Renewable Energy Institute after the 2011 Fukushima disaster, and tied to the **Gobitec** concept of Gobi-Desert solar/wind — would have linked Russia, Mongolia, China, Korea and Japan. SoftBank, State Grid (China), KEPCO (Korea) and Rosseti (Russia) signed an MoU in 2016.  It is now politically dead: the Renewable Energy Institute withdrew from GEIDCO in March 2024,  and the geopolitics of Russia, China, Korea and Japan make it unviable for the foreseeable future.

### 5. Brazil — continental-scale long-distance transmission

Brazil mirrors China’s geography (renewables far from load) and is the second real-world UHVDC case. The two **Belo Monte ±800 kV bipoles** carry power from the 11.2 GW Belo Monte hydro plant in Pará:  Bipole I (Belo Monte–Estreito, ~2,092 km, commissioned 2017) and Bipole II (Belo Monte–Rio de Janeiro/Xingu–Rio, ~2,539 km, 4 GW, commissioned 2019 — the world’s longest ±800 kV line), crossing 81 cities and five states. Both were built and are operated by State Grid of China under the Belt and Road Initiative. The earlier **Rio Madeira HVDC** links (~2,375 km, ±600 kV) carry Santo Antônio/Jirau hydro from Rondônia to São Paulo. Brazil spans multiple time zones; the lines prove the long-distance physics, though they carry hydro rather than solar.

### 6. United States — fragmented grid, ambitious studies

The US is split into **three asynchronous interconnections** — Eastern, Western and ERCOT (Texas) — plus Quebec, joined only by small back-to-back DC ties (about 1,320 MW between East and West via seven back-to-back ties, against ~700,000 MW of Eastern and ~250,000 MW of Western generating capacity). This split is historical/regulatory (ERCOT stays intrastate to avoid federal FERC jurisdiction; an early East–West synchronisation failed because the systems were too large to lock in phase), not a physical necessity.

**NREL’s Interconnections Seam Study** (Bloom, Novacheck, Brinkman, McCalley et al., 2020–2021) modelled HVDC connections across the East–West seam and found benefit-to-cost ratios ranging across scenarios — NREL’s public summaries cite roughly 1.2 to 2.5, with the technical report reporting that “the results show benefit-to-cost ratios that reach as high as 2.9.” It documents a strong **diurnal cross-seam flow**: in the afternoon, Western solar flows east into the Eastern peak; as the sun sets on the West Coast, the flow reverses, delivering Midwestern wind and Eastern thermal power back to the West — a direct demonstration of time-zone load-following within one country.

**Alexander MacDonald’s NOAA study** (MacDonald, Clack et al., “Future cost-competitive electricity systems and their impact on US CO2 emissions,” *Nature Climate Change* 2016) used NOAA weather data at 13-km/60-minute resolution to model a national HVDC grid connecting the best wind and solar sites to 256 markets. In its low-cost-renewables/high-cost-gas scenario, it found US electricity-sector CO₂ could fall “78 percent below 1990 levels” within 15 years at roughly 10 cents/kWh without raising costs (mid-range 61%; high-cost-renewables case 33%), by exploiting the fact that “the wind is always blowing somewhere or the sun is shining”  across the continent. Real projects advancing this logic include **SunZia** (New Mexico wind → Arizona/California, ±525 kV HVDC, ~885 km/550 mi, 3 GW, ~$11 bn, commercial operation ~2025–2026), **TransWest Express** (Wyoming wind → Nevada, ±600 kV, ~1,170 km, 3 GW, under construction), and the cancelled **Plains & Eastern Clean Line** and never-built **Tres Amigas** superstation.

### 7. Other major real/proposed cross-time-zone links

- **Sun Cable / Australia–Asia Power Link (AAPowerLink):** Northern Territory solar+battery → Darwin → Singapore via ~4,300 km subsea HVDC (the proposed world’s longest).  It collapsed in early 2023, was acquired by Cannon-Brookes’ Grok Ventures,  won Singapore conditional approval (1.75 GW) in October 2024,  and had Major Project Status renewed in July 2025  — but targets a final investment decision in 2027  and first power to Singapore only in the mid-2030s. Still alive but much delayed and de-scoped.
- **Xlinks Morocco–UK:** ~3,800 km subsea HVDC carrying ~10.5 GW of Moroccan solar+wind (3.6 GW firm, ~20 hours/day) to Devon. **Rejected by the UK government on 26 June 2025**, with energy minister Michael Shanks telling Parliament that “the Government have concluded that it is not in the UK national interest at this time to continue further consideration of support for the Morocco-UK power project,” citing it as “a first of a kind mega project which has a high level of inherent, cumulative risk (delivery, operational, and security).” Chair Sir Dave Lewis said the firm was “bitterly disappointed” and is seeking alternatives. The link is primarily north–south resource arbitrage (Moroccan sun, sun-tracking PV for morning/evening output) more than time-zone arbitrage.
- **Desertec:** the 2009 vision to power Europe from Saharan CSP/PV via HVDC; its industrial arm (Dii) shrank from 17 partners to 3 by end-2014 and the grand export project collapsed amid cost, governance, sovereignty and security concerns.
- **EuroAsia / Great Sea Interconnector** (Israel–Cyprus–Crete–Greece, ~1,208 km, 1–2 GW) and **EuroAfrica** (Egypt–Cyprus–Greece, ~1,396 km, 2 GW): EU Projects of Common Interest, partly funded (EuroAsia received €657 million from the Connecting Europe Facility), but both delayed/paused — EuroAsia by Turkish objections and financing disputes in 2025.

### 8. The honest critiques and obstacles

- **Capital cost and stranded-asset risk:** intercontinental cables run into tens of billions; GEIDCO’s own backbone vision is around US$4 million/km over ~175,000+ km. Merchant financing is hard because revenues depend on volatile arbitrage; long-term contracts may not be profitable — a caveat the Global Grid authors themselves flag.
- **Geopolitical dependency / the “energy weapon”:** relying on another nation for baseload power is a sovereignty risk (Lilliestam & Ellenbeck explicitly studied whether Desertec would make Europe vulnerable to the “energy weapon”). The UK’s Xlinks rejection cited the security of a cable passing multiple states’ waters; Desertec foundered partly on North-African political risk and the closed Algeria–Morocco border.
- **Single points of failure / resilience:** very large interconnections can propagate cascading failures and concentrate risk in a few high-value corridors that are attractive sabotage/cyber targets. Grid planners design to “N-1” security, but multi-failure resilience on giant wide-area grids is costly and rarely fully implemented.
- **Governance and sovereignty:** a global grid implies global market rules, a “global system operator” and dispute mechanisms — and GEIDCO’s China-centric leadership raises concerns (voiced by the Atlantic Council and Dialogue Earth) about who sets standards and the strategic leverage that control of intercontinental infrastructure confers.
- **Batteries may beat transmission for the daily shift:** the strongest technical critique is that lithium-ion (and emerging sodium-ion/long-duration) storage costs have fallen so fast that storing midday solar for the local evening peak is increasingly cheaper and more sovereign than building thousands of kilometres of cable. Time-zone transmission competes directly with — and on the daily cycle increasingly loses to — local storage, even as it retains advantages for weather/seasonal smoothing and accessing the very best remote resources. The Global Grid authors themselves concede price-arbitrage opportunities may shrink as near-zero-marginal-cost renewables proliferate.

## Recommendations

**For policymakers / system planners:**

1. **Prioritise large intra-national and trusted-neighbour HVDC overlays first.** The proven, financeable wins are domestic (China, Brazil, US SunZia/TransWest) and within trusted blocs (Nordics, EU). Pursue a US East–West–ERCOT macrogrid/seam upgrade: NREL’s benefit-cost ratios (up to 2.9 in the technical report) and the documented diurnal cross-seam flows make this the highest-value, lowest-geopolitical-risk move.
1. **Treat intercontinental, ocean-crossing links as case-by-case merchant/strategic bets, not as a “global grid” program.** Support them only where (a) the resource gain is large (e.g., Sahara/Australian solar), (b) governance and security are robust, and (c) they complement rather than replace domestic storage.
1. **Co-optimise transmission and storage rather than choosing one.** Use storage for the daily solar-to-evening shift and transmission for weather/seasonal/geographic diversity and tapping premier remote resources.

**Benchmarks that would change the recommendation:**

- If 4-hour-plus battery storage falls below roughly US$50/kWh installed, the case for *daily* time-zone transmission weakens sharply — lean further toward local storage.
- If a flagship ocean link (Sun Cable) reaches a final investment decision and operates reliably at its quoted price, treat subsea time-zone arbitrage as bankable and revisit other corridors.
- If converter/cable costs or losses materially improve (e.g., proven >3 GW, multi-thousand-km links below ~1.5–3% loss/1,000 km at lower €/km), raise the priority of longer links.
- A durable security/governance framework for cross-border power (enforceable transit rights, N-1 redundancy on critical corridors) would materially de-risk supergrids and should be a precondition for major new intercontinental commitments.

## Caveats

- **Distinguish what exists from what is proposed.** Only intra-national UHVDC (China, Brazil) and short cross-border interconnectors operate at scale today. *No* intercontinental, time-zone-bridging link is yet built. GEIDCO’s planet-spanning grid, Desertec and the Asia Super Grid are visions — several already defunct.
- **Most cited “time-zone arbitrage” benefits are modelled, not realised.** Figures from Guo et al., MacDonald/NOAA and the Global Grid paper come from scenario models with explicit assumptions; the authors themselves call several estimates “rough.”
- **The Belo Monte and Rio Madeira lines carry hydro, not solar** — they prove the long-distance physics but are not solar-time-zone arbitrage per se. China’s lines carry a mix of coal, hydro, wind and solar.
- **Some sources are promotional.** GEIDCO/State Grid and project developers (Sun Cable, Xlinks) have clear incentives to present optimistic figures; cost and security concerns from governments and the energy-security literature are a needed counterweight.
- **Numbers vary by source** (e.g., Changji–Guquan cited as 3,293–3,324 km; Belo Monte II as 2,518–2,543 km; UHVDC loss per 1,000 km from ~1.5% to ~3%); ranges reflect rounding, route-vs-line-length differences, and differing measurement assumptions.
